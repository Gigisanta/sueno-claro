# Market research synthesis

## Executive conclusion

The strongest opportunity is not a full sleep tracker. It is a web-first, privacy-first sleep timing utility: a better sleepcalculator.com with modern UI, bilingual SEO, PWA installability, and an optional mobile premium layer.

## Why this wedge is attractive

1. Search intent already exists: users search for sleep calculators, bedtime calculators, wake-up calculators, nap calculators, and sleep cycle calculators.
2. The core utility is deterministic and cheap to operate.
3. Full sleep trackers compete on sensors, microphone access, AI coaching, and long-term analytics; this product competes on speed, clarity, privacy, and trust.
4. RevenueCat Health & Fitness benchmarks show strong category economics, but a simple calculator should not copy aggressive hard-paywall apps because web SEO and trust are the top-of-funnel.
5. The product can monetize in layers: ads/affiliate on web, then RevenueCat premium for app conveniences.

## Competitive map

| Segment | Examples | Strength | Weakness / opening |
|---|---|---|---|
| Simple calculators | sleepcalculator.com, Calculator.net | Fast, search-indexed | Dated UX, shallow personalization, no app ecosystem |
| Editorial calculators | Sleep Foundation, Sleepopolis | Authority, content | Heavy content/affiliate feel, slower utility loop |
| Full trackers | Sleep Cycle, Sleep Time | Rich app features, smart alarm, retention | Requires trust/permissions; too heavy for quick calculation |
| Generic wellness apps | Calm, Headspace | Brand + content | Not focused on sleep timing calculation |

## Positioning

Primary positioning:

> Calculate your best sleep and wake times in seconds. No account. No microphone. No tracking.

Spanish positioning:

> Calculá cuándo dormir o despertarte para no cortar un ciclo. Sin cuenta, sin micrófono, sin vueltas.

## Target users

1. People with a fixed wake-up deadline: school, work, travel, appointments.
2. People about to sleep now who want the best alarm time.
3. Nappers: quick nap vs full-cycle nap.
4. Shift workers and irregular schedules.
5. Privacy-conscious wellness users who dislike microphone/sensor trackers.
6. SEO visitors who want an answer immediately.

## Jobs to be done

- “I need to wake at 7:00; tell me when to go to bed.”
- “I am going to sleep now; tell me when to set my alarm.”
- “I have 6 hours; what is my least-bad option?”
- “I want a nap but not grogginess.”
- “I want a recurring reminder for my bedtime.”

## Strategic risks

| Risk | Severity | Mitigation |
|---|---:|---|
| SEO competition | High | Programmatic long-tail pages, bilingual focus, fast UX, strong internal linking |
| Product too simple for subscription | Medium | Premium is convenience, not core: widgets, reminders, profiles, no ads, settings |
| Medical claim exposure | Medium | Wellness-only language, disclaimers, authoritative citations |
| Ads damage trust | Medium | Ads only after result; no interstitial/video/overlay |
| App store conversion weak | Medium | Use web validation before native app; iOS only after traction |

## Recommendation

Launch as a free web/PWA utility first. Add iOS only after SEO/content/ad metrics show repeated use or enough direct demand. RevenueCat should be integrated in the iOS phase, not before web validation.
